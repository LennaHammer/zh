# IELTS Speaking
Of course! I’ll give you simple, practical, IELTS Speaking-specific tips that actually work for band 6.5–7.5+ ✨
I’ll keep it easy to understand and use right away.
1. Speak MORE, don’t stop short
• Don’t give 1-word or 1-sentence answers.
• Use this formula:
Answer → Reason → Example → Extra detail
Example (Part 1):
Q: Do you like reading?
A: Yes, I really enjoy reading, because it helps me relax after a long day. I usually read novels before bed, especially mystery stories.
2. Don’t memorise whole scripts
Examiners can tell immediately and you’ll lose marks.
Instead:
• Memorise useful phrases (not full stories)
◦ To be honest,…
◦ Well,…
◦ Actually,…
◦ Let me think,…
◦ From my perspective,…
3. Improve Part 2 the most (it’s 2-3 mins long)
• You have 1 minute to take notes – use it!
• Write key words only, not full sentences.
• Structure your 2-min talk clearly:
4. Introduction (what it is/who with/when)
5. Details (what you did/saw/felt)
6. Feelings & opinion (why it was special)
7. Use better vocabulary (not hard words)
• Replace simple words:
very good → fantastic / brilliant
very bad → terrible / awful
very happy → delighted / thrilled
important → crucial / significant
8. Show you can use different grammar tenses
Try to use:
• Past simple (talk about experiences)
• Present perfect (life experiences)
• Future plans (will / going to / plan to)
• Conditionals (If I had more time, I would…)
This raises your grammar score a lot.
9. Fluency trick: buy time when you’re stuck
If you forget a word or need to think:
• “Let me think for a second…”
• “What I mean is…”
• “It’s kind of like…”
Don’t stay silent – silence kills fluency marks.
10. Practice every day (even 10 mins)
• Record yourself answering IELTS speaking questions
• Listen back: Did you pause too much? Did you repeat words?

🗣️ IELTS Speaking High-Frequency Phrases


1. 1. Opening / Filler phrases (to avoid silence) • Well, … • To be honest, … • Actually, … • Let me think for a second… • That’s an interesting question. • I haven’t really thought about that before, but…
2. 2. Giving opinions • I think that… • In my opinion,… • From my perspective,… • Personally, I feel that… • I’d say that…

4. 4. Giving examples • For instance,… • For example,… • Like,… • Such as…
5. 5. Adding extra details (to make your answer longer) • Especially… • In particular,… • As far as I’m concerned,… • Whenever I can, I…
6. 6. Talking about past experiences • I remember when… • When I was younger,… • I’ve been… • I used to… • One time,…
7. 7. Talking about the future • I plan to… • I’m hoping to… • I probably will… • If I have time, I’ll… • In the future, I’d like to…

## Paraphrasing

Absolutely! This is called paraphrasing — it’s super important for IELTS Speaking (especially Part 2 & 3) to show you have a wide vocabulary and don’t repeat simple words.
I’ll give you common simple words that learners overuse, plus better, natural replacements with real speaking examples — exactly like you’d use in the IELTS test.


1. Very + adjective (stop saying “very” all the time!)
	Instead of: • very good • very bad • very happy • very tired • very big • very small
	Use these:
	• excellent / fantastic ✅ Original: The food was very good. ✅ Better: The food was excellent.
	• terrible / awful ✅ Original: The weather was very bad. ✅ Better: The weather was awful.
	• delighted / thrilled ✅ Original: I was very happy. ✅ Better: I was thrilled.
	• exhausted ✅ Original: I was very tired after work. ✅ Better: I was exhausted after work.
	• huge / massive ✅ Original: There was a very big crowd. ✅ Better: There was a huge crowd.
	• tiny ✅ Original: The phone was very small. ✅ Better: The phone was tiny.


2. Overused basic verbs
	- Think → believe / feel / reckon / suppose ✅ Simple: I think city life is stressful. ✅ Better: I reckon city life is quite stressful. ✅ Better: I believe people should protect the environment.
	- Like → enjoy / love / be keen on / be fond of ✅ Simple: I like reading books. ✅ Better: I enjoy reading books in my free time. ✅ Better: I’m keen on watching documentaries.
	- Want → would like / hope to / wish to ✅ Simple: I want to travel abroad. ✅ Better: I would like to travel abroad next year.
	- Make → cause / create / lead to ✅ Simple: Stress makes people tired. ✅ Better: Stress causes people to feel exhausted.
	- Help → assist / support ✅ Simple: My parents help me a lot. ✅ Better: My parents support me a lot with my studies.


3. Common nouns
	- Thing → item / object / aspect / issue ✅ Simple: One important thing is education. ✅ Better: One important aspect is education. ✅ Simple: I bought many things. ✅ Better: I bought several items.
	- People → individuals / residents / citizens ✅ Simple: Many people live in cities. ✅ Better: Many residents choose to live in urban areas.
	- Problem → issue / challenge / difficulty ✅ Simple: There are many problems with tourism. ✅ Better: There are many issues with mass tourism.


4. Linking words (simple → advanced)
	- And → plus / furthermore / also ✅ Simple: I like reading and I like sports. ✅ Better: I like reading; plus, I enjoy playing sports.
	- But → however / yet / though ✅ Simple: The city is busy but exciting. ✅ Better: The city is busy; however, it’s really exciting.
	- So → therefore / that’s why ✅ Simple: I was tired so I went to bed early. ✅ Better: I was exhausted; that’s why I went to bed early.


5. Full natural IELTS-style example
	- Simple version (too basic for high band): “I think working from home is very good. People can save time and they don’t need to spend money on transport. But sometimes it’s very boring and people can be very lazy.”
	- Improved version (paraphrased, band 7+): “I believe working from home is extremely beneficial. Individuals can save plenty of time and avoid spending money on transport. However, it can sometimes be monotonous, and people may become unmotivated.”

## Part 3 Questions

Here are high-quality, realistic IELTS Speaking Part 3 questions grouped by common IELTS topics (the most frequently tested ones in the exam):
1. Work & Employment
	1. Do you think people today work longer hours than in the past? Why?
	2. What are the advantages and disadvantages of working from home?
	3. Why do some people choose to change jobs frequently?
	4. How important is job satisfaction compared to salary?
	5. Should employers provide training for their staff? Why?
2. Education
	1. What are the benefits of studying abroad?
	2. Do you think children should learn many subjects or focus on fewer subjects?
	3. How has technology changed the way students learn?
	4. Should schools teach practical skills like cooking or money management?
	5. What is the role of parents in a child’s education?
3. Technology & the Internet
	1. How has social media changed the way people communicate?
	2. What are the drawbacks of overusing smartphones?
	3. Do you think technology makes people more or less social?
	4. How can we protect children from harmful online content?
	5. Will technology replace teachers in the future?
4. Environment
	1. What can individuals do to protect the environment?
	2. Should governments be responsible for solving environmental problems?
	3. Why do people often ignore environmental issues?
	4. Is it better to use public transport or private cars? Why?
	5. How does tourism affect the natural environment?
5. Cities & Living Places
	1. What are the advantages of living in a big city compared to the countryside?
	2. Why are more people moving to cities nowadays?
	3. How can cities be made more environmentally friendly?
	4. Do you think city life is more stressful than rural life?
	5. What makes a city a good place to live?
6. Food & Diet
	1. How have people’s eating habits changed in recent years?
	2. Why is fast food so popular around the world?
	3. Should governments tax unhealthy food?
	4. Is home cooking better than eating out? Why?
	5. How important is it for children to learn about healthy eating?
7. Leisure & Free Time
	1. Do people today have more or less free time than before?
	2. What are the benefits of having hobbies?
	3. Why do some people prefer to spend free time alone?
	4. How does entertainment (like movies/TV) influence society?
	5. Should children spend more time playing outdoors than indoors?
8. Travel & Tourism
	1. What are the benefits of international tourism for a country?
	2. How has cheap air travel affected the world?
	3. Do you think travel makes people more open-minded?
	4. What problems can mass tourism cause?
	5. Why do people like to visit historical places?

## Giving Examples

Great question! This is the #1 secret to Band 7+ and 8 in Part 3 — most students know how to give an example, but they give boring, weak examples that don’t help their score.
I’ll teach you exactly HOW to give a perfect Part 3 example (simple, fast, high-scoring, no stress) — with formulas you can memorise.
First: Why examples matter in Part 3
Part 3 is abstract, academic questions (not about your personal life like Part 1).
If you only say general things, you sound shallow.
→ Example = proof that you understand the topic deeply
This directly raises your score for:
✅ Fluency & Coherence
✅ Lexical Resource (vocab)
✅ Band 7, 7.5, 8


3 Types of Perfect Part 3 Examples (use ANY of these)
You don’t need real true stories — you can make up 100% realistic examples. Examiners don’t check if it’s true, only if it’s natural.
1. Personal / Someone you know example (easiest for you)
	Formula:
	For example, my + [friend/cousin/colleague/teacher] + who + action + what happened + result
	Examples you can copy:
	• For example, my cousin works in an office, and he has to commute two hours every day.
	• For instance, my little sister does group projects at school, and she’s learned how to share ideas with classmates.
	👉 Super easy, you can always invent someone you “know”.
2. General people / society example (more mature = Band 8)
	Formula:
	For example, many + [people/employees/students/parents] + in + [place] + often + what they do
	Samples:
	• For example, many office workers in big cities waste a huge amount of time travelling to work.
	• For instance, a lot of students nowadays use online tools to study from home.
3. Compare past & present (Band 8 favourite — very impressive)
	Formula:
	For example, in the past, people + … but now, they + …
	Samples:
	• For example, in the past, people only used phones to talk, but now they can have video meetings online.
	• For instance, in the past, teamwork wasn’t taught much at school, but now almost every class has group activities.

What makes a BAD example (avoid these!)
❌ Too short:
“For example, me.” → useless, no score
❌ Too long, storytelling like a movie:
“Last Monday I woke up, then I ate breakfast, then I took bus…” → examiner gets bored
❌ Not related to the question:
Question about teamwork → you talk about your holiday → off-topic


2 Magic Phrases to introduce examples (memorise these!)
Use these every time you give an example — they sound Band 8 automatically:
4. For example, … (most common, safe)
5. For instance, … (a bit more academic, great for Part 3)
6. Take … for example (also really good)
